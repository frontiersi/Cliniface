Anthropometric Analysis

Cliniface takes measurements based on the positions of standard landmarks. These measurements are compared against known statistics of facial growth available from the research literature. This section describes how the various facial measurements are taken and then how they are analysed to infer the presence of atypical facial traits.

Measurement types

Cliniface takes five different kinds of measurement which are calculated from the spatial positions of the facial landmarks (or weighted combinations of the landmark positions). Each metric type is described below in turn.

Angle

Angle measurements are always taken parallel to one of the canonical anatomical facial planes. They are defined by three points: two outer points and an inner point at which the angle is measured as the arc subtended by the two line segments that meet at the inner point and with end-points at the outer points. The angle is single dimensioned and is shown in degrees.

Asymmetry

Asymmetry is measured as signed distance in the X, Y, and Z axes representing asymmetry through the medial, transverse, and coronal planes respectively, and as overall magnitude (four dimensional). For a bi-lateral landmark pair {p,q} with p on the right lateral and q on the left, calculation is as follows: p is reflected through the medial plane to point p'. Difference vector d = q - p' is taken, the components of which correspond to asymmetry along the respective axes. Magnitude is as the L2 norm of d. Because the values corresponding to the axial components are signed, positive values denote outgrowth in the positive halfspace (or ingrowth in the negative halfspace) through the respective plane.

Depth

The dimension of depth measurements is not fixed. A depth measurement is taken from a point in space above the surface of the face to the surface of the face. The direction in which depth is measured depends upon the measurement but can be in-plane (parallel with an anatomical facial plane) in which case the measurement is marked "[IN-PLANE]" in the bottom left of the main viewer or at right angles to a line segment defined between two points. See here for more information about in-plane measurements.

Distance

Distance measurements are one dimensional straight-line distances between pairs of landmarks (or weighted combinations of landmarks).

Region

Region measurements are one dimensional and are a measure of the ratio of the square of a region's perimeter with its area. Currently, the sole measurement of this kind in Cliniface is Upper Lip Circularity.

Automated facial analysis

The different measurements described above are automatically taken from the 3D facial image whenever the positions of related landmarks on the face change. Every time this happens or the subject's demographic information changes, Cliniface updates its analysis by comparing the measurements to statistics matched to the subject. These results are then forwarded through to an objective criteria checking scheme for predefined facial traits of clinical significance. If the criteria conditions are met for any of these traits then they are automatically flagged as being present for the selected subject.

Measurements browser

After performing face / landmark detection, the Measurements Browser (shown below) can be accessed from the Metrics menu.

Upon its opening, the measurements are also visualised graphically in the main viewer on the loaded faces. Highlight a metric by clicking its row in the table or using the up and down arrow keys. This highlights the measurement on the faces and updates the text in the bottom of the viewers to show that measurement's values for the respective faces (note that if two faces are in a single viewer then no measurement data is shown).

Some measurements are multi-dimensional (e.g., asymmetry) with the values for each dimension shown from left to right in the row marked "Measure". Measurements with associated growth curve data show the corresponding Z-scores underneath. Not all measurements have growth curve data, and many measurements have growth curve data only for people of European ethnic descent. Any mismatches in demographic data with the available measurement statistics are shown alongside the measurement values.

The table of measurements can be filtered by measurement type, anatomical region, and related HPO term (filtering by related syndrome is disabled in the current version).

Automatic statistics matching

Cliniface tries to automatically match the demographics of the facial image to growth curve statistics from the those available for each measurement. The closest match is used if an exact match isn't found, and this may result in there being a demographic mismatch for the selected measurement and subject. If this is the case, the nature of the mismatch is shown in the bottom left of the main viewer alongside the measurement values.

To disable automatic statistics matching, uncheck the Subject Match checkbox in the measurements browser. This enables manual selection of statistics for the currently selected measurement using the dropdown lists for sex, ethnicity, and source (where more than one source of growth curve data is available for the selected sex / ethnicity combination). Checking the Subject Match checkbox resets the chosen statistics for that measurement. Note that leaving this checkbox unchecked for any measurement will prevent Cliniface from automatically selecting the most appropriate set of growth curve statistics for the currently selected face! For this reason it is usually best to recheck this box after exploring the available statistics for a given measurement.

Growth curve charts

Click the graph button in the bottom right of the Measurements Browser to see a graph of the growth curve statistics for the highlighted measurement. The graph shows the growth curve distribution of that measurement for the current source of data. The markers on the graph show the subject's measurement as a round coloured dot (or dots for bilateral measurements). Hollow dot(s) indicate that the subject is outside the age domain of the growth curve.

The graph shows the mean of the measurement and +/- 1 and 2 standard deviations by age. If the measurement is multi-valued, select which value dimension to view from the dropdown list on the bottom left of the chart dialog. The chart can be saved as an image by clicking the adjacent save button. In-plane measurements

Different sources of the statistics used in Cliniface have used different methods to take the same measurements. For example, most inter-landmark distances are measured as the length of the straight line segment between the respective landmark point pairs. However, the standard clinical protocol for measuring some inter-landmark distances requires measuring the length of the projection of the connecting line segment into one of the standard facial planes. In Cliniface, this is termed an in-plane measurement. Some types of measurement such as angles are always taken in-plane.

In-plane measurements are also a potential source of error: if the ends of a manual measurement device (e.g., a ruler) are not held close to and equidistantly from the face, this will result in the reported distance being shorter than in actuality. Therefore it is important that all measurement statistics used in Cliniface are clear about how they have been obtained. Cliniface uses only statistics where the measuring protocol has been clearly explained in the associated published literature.

The growth curve statistics for a given measurement specify whether they have been generated from in-plane measurements or not. Cliniface takes in-plane measurements if the matched set of statistics is itself derived from in-plane measurements and will switch measurement mode automatically to match set set source of statistics.

In the example below, the automatically matched statistics for Nasal Height are from the FaceBase website which provides statistics for this measurement as a point-to-point distance between the subnasale and nasion landmarks.

In the image below, having manually changed the measurement statistics to a set generated from in-plane measurements, Cliniface shows the measurement taken in the corresponding fashion with the distance projected into the coronal plane. This foreshortens the distance appropriately to match the statistics being compared against.

If the measurement is taken in-plane due to the selected statistics the flag "[IN-PLANE]" is displayed next to the measurement values. To override Cliniface from automatically matching the mode in which the measurement is taken to the selected statistics, check / uncheck the In-plane checkbox in the Measurements Browser. This can only be changed if the Subject Match checkbox is unchecked since manually setting the statistics to compare against is not usually appropriate since there's a risk of mismatching the subject's demographics. If the mode of measurement (in or out of plane) does not match the mode used by the selected statistics, the flag "[IN-PLANE MISMATCH]" is displayed next to the measurement values.

Atypical features

Depressing the large warning triangle button in the bottom right of the Measurements Browser restricts the visible set of measurements to only those that are related to facial traits that are found to be atypical according to their objective criteria given the currently set statistics.

Depressing this button also restricts the terms listed in the Human Phenotype Ontology (HPO) Browser to those that fulfill the criteria for being found on the subject's face. This list of HPO terms is also updated in the Assessment Information dialog (see here for details).

Human Phenotype Ontology browser

The Human Phenotype Ontology (HPO) Browser (shown below) can be accessed from the Metrics menu. This shows the table of HPO terms that Cliniface has objective criteria for and can detect in principle.

HPO terms can be filtered by anatomical region. The main table shows each term on its own row with the currently selected term highlighted. Click a different row or use the up and down arrow keys to show information related to each term. The columns from left to right respectively give the official identifier, the name of the term, and the related measurements. Beneath the table are remarks about the selected term including any synonyms plus any references. Below the synonyms is a link to the online website where details about the term and its related syndromes and genes can be found.

To the right of this is given the objective and the subjective identifying criteria for the selected trait. The objective criteria is what Cliniface uses to classify the presence of the trait and this describes the measurement(s) used to discover whether or not the trait is present. Underneath this are the subjective criteria which describe the observations that a clinician might typically use to determine if the trait is present or not.
